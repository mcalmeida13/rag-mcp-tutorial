# Honeybee

Honeybees (genus Apis) are social insects that live in colonies of tens of thousands of individuals, organized around a single reproductive queen, thousands of sterile female workers, and a smaller number of male drones. Colonies function almost like a single organism, a concept biologists call a "superorganism."

Worker honeybees communicate the location of food sources to their hive-mates through the waggle dance, a figure-eight movement whose angle relative to the sun indicates direction and whose duration indicates distance. This behavior, first decoded by Karl von Frisch, remains one of the most studied examples of animal communication.

A single honeybee will produce only about one-twelfth of a teaspoon of honey in its lifetime, but the collective effort of a hive can produce tens of kilograms of surplus honey in a good season. Honeybees are also critical pollinators, responsible for pollinating roughly a third of the crops humans eat, including many fruits, nuts, and vegetables.

Honeybee colonies face significant threats from a phenomenon known as colony collapse disorder, as well as from pesticides, habitat loss, and parasitic mites such as Varroa destructor.
