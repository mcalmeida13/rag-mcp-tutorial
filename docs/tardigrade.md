# Tardigrade

Tardigrades, sometimes called water bears or moss piglets, are microscopic eight-legged animals typically less than a millimeter long. They live almost everywhere on Earth, from deep ocean trenches to mountaintops, but are most commonly found in the thin films of water on mosses and lichens.

Tardigrades are famous as one of the most extremophile-tolerant animals known. Through a process called cryptobiosis, a tardigrade can expel almost all the water from its body, curl into a dehydrated ball called a "tun," and suspend its metabolism to near zero. In this state, tardigrades have survived temperatures near absolute zero, pressures several times greater than the deepest ocean trench, intense radiation, and even the vacuum of outer space during a 2007 European Space Agency experiment.

A tardigrade can remain in a tun state for years, or even decades in some documented cases, and rehydrate back to normal activity within hours once moisture returns. Despite this resilience, tardigrades are not indestructible in normal daily life; outside the tun state they are vulnerable to predators and require a film of water to move and feed at all.

Scientists study tardigrades' unique proteins, including one nicknamed "Dsup" (damage suppressor), for potential applications in protecting human cells from radiation damage.
