# Narwhal

The narwhal (Monodon monoceros) is a medium-sized toothed whale that lives year-round in the Arctic waters of Canada, Greenland, Norway, and Russia. It is best known for the long, spiraled tusk that protrudes from the head of most males, which is in fact an elongated canine tooth that can grow up to three meters long.

For centuries the narwhal's tusk was traded as unicorn horn, fetching enormous prices in medieval Europe before naturalists confirmed its true origin. Modern research suggests the tusk is a sensory organ, riddled with nerve endings that can detect changes in water temperature, pressure, and the salinity of the surrounding seawater.

Narwhals are deep divers, capable of descending more than 1,500 meters below the surface in pursuit of prey such as Greenland halibut, cod, and squid, often under thick sea ice. They typically travel in pods of several individuals and are highly vocal, using clicks, whistles, and pulsed calls to communicate and navigate via echolocation in the dark polar waters.

Narwhals are considered near threatened, with climate change reshaping the sea ice they depend on and increasing shipping traffic disturbing their historically remote habitat.
