# Platypus

The platypus (Ornithorhynchus anatinus) is a semi-aquatic mammal native to eastern Australia and Tasmania, and one of only five living species of monotreme, mammals that lay eggs instead of giving birth to live young. When the first platypus specimen reached European scientists in 1799, many suspected it was an elaborate hoax stitched together from parts of other animals.

A platypus has a duck-like bill, a beaver-like tail, otter-like webbed feet, and dense waterproof fur. The bill is not just for show: it is packed with electroreceptors that let the platypus detect the faint electrical fields generated by the muscle contractions of prey while hunting underwater with its eyes, ears, and nostrils all sealed shut.

Male platypuses are among the very few venomous mammals in the world. They have a sharp spur on each hind leg connected to a venom gland, used mainly during the breeding season to compete with rival males; the venom causes severe pain in humans but is not considered lethal.

Despite laying eggs, platypuses nurse their young with milk, which is secreted through pores in the skin rather than through nipples, since monotremes lack them entirely.
