# Octopus

Octopuses are soft-bodied, eight-limbed molluscs belonging to the order Octopoda. They are found in every ocean, from coral reefs to the deep sea, and are known for their remarkable intelligence, problem-solving ability, and capacity to escape enclosures that seem completely sealed.

An octopus has three hearts: two pump blood through the gills, while the third circulates it to the rest of the body. Their blood is blue, colored by a copper-based molecule called hemocyanin rather than the iron-based hemoglobin found in vertebrate blood, which is more efficient at transporting oxygen in cold, low-oxygen water.

Octopuses can change both the color and texture of their skin almost instantly, using specialized cells called chromatophores, to camouflage against their surroundings or to communicate. Most species also have no internal or external skeleton, allowing them to squeeze through any gap larger than their beak, the only hard part of their body.

Despite their intelligence, most octopus species live only one to two years, and females typically die shortly after their eggs hatch, having stopped eating to guard them the entire time.
