# Peacock Mantis Shrimp

The peacock mantis shrimp (Odontodactylus scyllarus) is a brightly colored marine crustacean found on coral reefs across the Indo-Pacific. Despite its name, it is neither a true shrimp nor related to praying mantises; it belongs to its own order, Stomatopoda, and is more closely related to crabs and lobsters.

This animal is best known for its extraordinarily fast punch. Its front claws are modified into club-like appendages that can strike prey with the acceleration of a .22 caliber bullet, among the fastest movements in the animal kingdom. The strike is so fast that it causes cavitation bubbles to form and collapse in the surrounding water, producing a secondary shockwave and a brief flash of light and heat, strong enough to crack aquarium glass or shatter the shells of crabs and snails.

Peacock mantis shrimp also have the most complex visual system known in any animal. Their eyes contain up to sixteen types of photoreceptors, compared to just three in humans, and can independently move and rotate to track prey. Researchers believe they can perceive both polarized light and a much broader range of the color spectrum than humans, including into the ultraviolet.

They are solitary and highly territorial, digging burrows in sand or rubble on the reef floor and aggressively defending them from intruders, including much larger animals.
