# Axolotl

The axolotl (Ambystoma mexicanum) is a neotenic salamander native to a small number of lakes and canals near Mexico City, most notably what remains of Lake Xochimilco. Neoteny means the axolotl retains its larval features, including external feathery gills and a fin running along its body, throughout its entire adult life instead of metamorphosing into a land-dwelling form like most salamanders.

Axolotls are famous among biologists for their extraordinary regenerative ability. An axolotl can regrow entire limbs, portions of its spinal cord, its heart, and even parts of its brain, all without forming scar tissue. This makes it one of the most important model organisms in regenerative medicine research, as scientists try to understand why axolotls can do this and humans largely cannot.

In the wild, axolotls are critically endangered, with wild populations reduced to a fraction of historical numbers due to urbanization, water pollution, and invasive species like tilapia and perch. Ironically, axolotls are extremely common in captivity, bred by the millions for research and as pets, in a wide range of colors including the natural mottled brown-green form, as well as leucistic (pale pink) and albino variants.
